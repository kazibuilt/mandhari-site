Why I love where I work

We’ve been talking to some of our partners in the sector, both in the UK and the US, for their take on current challenges and opportunities, from the benefits of clustering to how to attract and keep talent.

Who wants to work in a business park?

We do, says managing partner Oliver Milton, now that owners are prioritising community and carbon over cars and convenience

For decades science and technology companies have been subjected to the dreary anonymity of out of town business parks. The requirements of the buildings they occupy have forced companies to locate in places that offer the right types of space, access and logistics, but nothing in terms of vibrancy, wellbeing or amenity.

The tide is turning. Occupiers are demanding so much more out of their workplace. At the GSK site in Stevenage we’re developing a masterplan that puts community and carbon ahead of cars and convenience. Buildings are organised around fantastic outdoors spaces. Amenity has been prioritised and located to maximise the benefits of clustering. Cars have been moved to the perimeter to prioritise the movement of people.

So whilst it is still an out of town location – with all of the benefits of transport connections and space for large technical buildings – it feels much more like it’s part of a city.

At Oxpens this idea of science in the city is taken one step further. We’ve developed a masterplan that supports a new mixed-use quarter for Oxford. Spaces for science and technology are cheek by jowl with homes and spaces for business and entertainment. The large format floor plans that we know can support laboratories efficiently and flexibly, have been designed into the masterplan in a way that retains human scaled external spaces.  It’s an exemplar of how science can exist alongside other uses, creating vibrancy and diversity within a new quarter of the city.

This trend from business parks, to campus-plus, to city quarter is set to continue with huge benefits for the occupiers and communities that form around these clusters.

Alistair Cory

"Collaboration is the holy grail."

The Agile Lab is a low-barrier entry membership lab that supports new innovation through providing a lab-enabled space for people to co-work. There are currently 16 new businesses based there, with 12 of them being spin-offs from the university.

There’s a great story about a serendipitous meeting in the Agile Lab. There were two brand-new companies based there; one of them is focused on organic chemistry, and the other start-up was led by an academic who was developing an external cream that breaks down gout crystals. But he wasn’t an expert in organic chemistry. It turns out that the other spin-out company was able to help, so they were employed to do a piece of research to move his organic cream one step further. It was a great accidental opportunity that came from simply sharing a lab.

Human interaction makes your day nicer, but collaboration is the holy grail.

Alistair Cory, Oxford University Science Park, Directorate department

Dr Kristin-Anne Rutter

"We are thinking about the facilities that make this the place that people want to be."

We have three key things that other places do not benefit from. We have very strong anchor institutions which, when you talk to people who are coming to locate on the campus, is driving that choice – whether it is the University of Cambridge, the Laboratory of Molecular Biology or the hospitals, AstraZeneca, or incoming research institutions.

Then, because it is a large site, the transport options particularly sustainable and public transport to the site. Work is already happening to create off road and dedicated public transport corridors to the site with the planned development of Cambridge South Station on the mainline to London.

And finally, we are thinking about the facilities that sit on top of that, that make this the place that people want to be and that really support collaboration. This is what our Vision 2050 is all about; recognizing that green spaces, leisure facilities and shops, have been lacking from our campus and we have just relied on the amazingness of the anchor institutions to carry it forward to this place.

Dr Kristin-Anne Rutter, Executive Director, Cambridge University Health Partners

John Sommerville

"There is potential for more manufacturing type space."

We are seeing a bit more development of good manufacturing practice (GMP) type space and it is set to grow. When you look to the US, we’ve seen companies developing new therapies that are based on personalised medicine, developing things that are specific to one person where you need the ability to develop that technology, but you also need the right space to then develop those therapies and get them to the places where they can be given to the patients.

I can see there is a potential need for more of that GMP type space, which is often, low rise industrial type space, in relative proximity to the to the clusters of R&D activity.

John Sommerville, Managing Partner, Creative Places

Angus Horner

"We appointed cluster managers who were professionally tasked with making each of the respective clusters go."

Clusters happen when a core group of people have been absolutely determined for many years to help their community grow. We codified it at Harwell tolerably well and it remains to this day a public sector private sector partnership, with a generosity and an openness in terms of that sharing relationship.

But we also had a cluster steering board. We appointed cluster managers who were professionally tasked with making each of the respective clusters go. Those cluster managers were definitely not real estate people; they were always drawn from a research side, from an industrial area that was relevant to the cluster they sat inside.

And we then built programmes around our clusters, whether that was marketing events or networking, or proof of concept calls for grant funding. It’s a very programmatic sort of basis for growing a cluster as opposed to a physical one.

Angus Horner, Co-Founder, Harwell Oxford Partners

Alistair Cory

"The development needs to be led by the specific science you’re developing."

There’s a gold rush mentality in response to the lack of space in the UK. There is a lack of the right kind of labs across the country, particularly in the southeast. While there certainly is a demand for biomed or life science spaces, there’s also a huge amount of other science and tech space that needs to be provided. The development needs to be led by the specific science you’re developing.

At Begbroke, we know exactly what type of science we’re enabling – all of which require a different type of environment.

The UK has no shortage of great ideas, and I’m always optimistic about our capacity to constantly create new endeavours. Our challenge is to retain and grow talent within Oxfordshire – we’re competing with the likes of the US where favourable investments are available.

Alistair Cory, Oxford University Science Park, Directorate department

Dr Kristin-Anne Rutter

"A building focused on addressing one of the biggest chronic health challenges we have as a nation."

The best example of clustering within our buildings is the new Heart Lung Research Institute – the physical embodiment of everything we are trying to achieve on campus. It has wet labs mixed with dry labs, mixed with computer labs. It allows researchers from different departments within the university to come together, all focused on how to improve heart and lung disease.

It has collaboration spaces and facilities to do clinical trials. So, you can go from an epidemiological model of a large cohort to the lab to test out that idea; back to designing an intervention, to testing it physically in the Heart Lung Research Institute clinical research facility.

One building focused on addressing one of the biggest chronic health challenges we have as a nation.

Dr Kristin-Anne Rutter, Executive Director, Cambridge University Health Partners

John Somerville

"The ability to host events and exhibitions shouldn't be underestimated."

I think that the one area that always seems to chime with occupiers is good café space. Where you can get a good coffee. Where there are places for informal working. You need to get the balance right between soft seating and tables and chairs because occupiers quite like to come and sit with a laptop and work.

And yes, it takes up lettable space. But I think if you do it well, you get added value through reducing letting periods and voids, particularly in R&D environments. The added benefit of how that creates interaction for your businesses and value add for them as a result, but also the ability to host events and exhibitions shouldn’t be underestimated.

John Sommerville, Managing Partner, Creative Places

Angus Horner

"Green infrastructure and the landscape can glue a place together."

It can be difficult to create a consistency of environment when buildings in larger schemes are so very different in type, form, and scale. We alighted on green infrastructure and the landscape as something that could glue a place together.

So many people want to be outside during the working day, we should focus amenities around external and natural capital. Not only is it healthy and good for productivity, but also from a financial perspective, when you are trying to build and steward big places, it costs a lot less to invest in green infrastructure than hard infrastructure.

Angus Horner, Co-Founder, Harwell Oxford Partners
